## **P2. 30-second summary**

This poster reports secondary analyses testing whether baseline **activity-supportive neighborhood environment features** predict **long-term changes in objectively measured physical activity** across the lifespan. Data came from four cross-sectional age-specific studies (children, adolescents, adults, older adults), with a fifth study re-recruiting participants for long-term follow-up (**5.1 to 15 years**) in a combined sample of **n=1067**. Moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA) was measured by **Actigraph accelerometer** at baseline and follow-up, and perceived neighborhood environment by **NEWS**, with **longitudinal structural path models** predicting follow-up MVPA from baseline environment features. Conclusions indicate MVPA declines over time in all age groups, but higher activity-supportive neighborhood features—across macro and micro scales—are associated with **less decline** in MVPA; people in high-supportive neighborhoods show less long-term MVPA decline than those in low-supportive neighborhoods.

## **P5. Results (exhaustive)**

### **P5.1 Key findings (bullet list)**

* MVPA declines from baseline to follow-up in all age groups.  
* Baseline activity-supportive neighborhood environment features are associated with less decline in MVPA across all age groups.  
* Specifically, “5 macro and 3 of 4 micro-scale” baseline activity-supportive features, and overall activity supportiveness, are associated with less MVPA decline.  
* Those living in high-activity supportive neighborhoods have less decline in long-term MVPA compared to those in low-activity supportive neighborhoods.  
* Implication: Findings augment evidence on neighborhood environment and PA, guiding policy and practice for NCD prevention/control across the lifespan.
