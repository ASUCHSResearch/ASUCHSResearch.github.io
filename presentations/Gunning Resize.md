## **P2. 30-second summary**

This poster describes a pilot study (“TREAD”) testing whether a time-restricted eating (TRE) intervention is feasible in patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and exploring effects on cognition and blood biomarkers relevant to Alzheimer’s disease (AD). In preliminary results, 27 participants completed the study and adherence to the targeted eating window was high (e.g., an 8-hour window achieved on 78.2% of days). The authors conclude the intervention appears feasible and that completing remaining participants will clarify impacts on cognition and AD-related blood biomarkers.

## **P6. Discussion & implications**

* **Authors’ stated conclusions:** “Preliminary observations from the TREAD trial indicate high feasibility and adherence to a TRE intervention among patients with MCI.” Also: “Completion of the remaining participants will shed light on the potential impact of TRE on cognition and blood biomarkers of AD.”  
* **Implications (poster-grounded):** The poster supports that implementing TRE with monitoring/support is feasible in this MCI sample and that further completion/analysis is needed to assess cognitive and biomarker effects.  
* **Mechanism/explanation (ONLY if stated):** Fasting leads to ketone production and metabolic switching from glucose to fatty acid/ketone utilization, triggering adaptive metabolic/cellular responses; additionally, cortical glucose uptake is decreased in AD while ketone uptake is not affected.  
* **Connections to prior work (ONLY if referenced):** “Cortical glucose uptake is decreased in Alzheimer’s disease (AD); ketone uptake is not affected.” “Little is known about the potential benefits of TRE in patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI).” (No citations/references listed on poster.)
