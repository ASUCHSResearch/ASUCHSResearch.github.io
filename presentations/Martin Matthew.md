## **P2. 30-second summary**

This poster presents a needs assessment of the care ecosystem for pregnant and parenting women (PPW) with substance use disorder (SUD) in Northern Arizona, described as compassionate but structurally fragile. Using semi-structured interviews across clinical, public health, justice, tribal leadership, and community perspectives, the authors identify fragmentation as the core system problem and emphasize that relationships (especially peers/paraprofessionals) are currently holding the system together. The poster proposes immediate, short-term, and long-term recommendations to standardize pathways, stabilize peer roles, improve coordination/data sharing, and align policy and payment structures.

## **P5. Results (exhaustive)**

### **P5.1 Key findings (bullet list)**

* The “core system problem” is fragmentation; relationships (not infrastructure) currently hold the system together.  
* Housing, peers, and cultural responsiveness drive engagement more than any single clinical service.  
* Identification and engagement rely on trust and peer outreach; screening protocols are inconsistent; fear/stigma (e.g., fear of child removal) limits disclosure.  
* Peers/paraprofessionals (CHWs, doulas, peer support specialists) act as system connectors and help compensate for clinician shortages while reducing stigma.  
* “Temporal mismatch”: pregnancy creates a time-sensitive intervention window that bureaucratic processes don’t match.  
* Policy/payer implications emphasize payment parity and tying payer pilots to avoided downstream costs (ED, NICU, foster care).  
* Recommendations propose a standardized priority pathway with 48–72-hour warm handoffs, navigator/peer single points of contact, and longer-term bundled payment and shared data infrastructure.

## **P6. Discussion & implications**

* **Authors’ stated conclusions:** Fragmentation is the core system problem; relationships (not infrastructure) currently hold the system together; sustainable change requires aligning people, policy, and payment.  
    
* **Implications (poster-grounded):**  
    
  * Practice/operations: Standardize screening and warm handoffs (48–72 hours) and assign navigators/peers as single contact points.  
  * Workforce: Stabilize paraprofessional roles via pilot Medicaid reimbursement and expand training (PSI, MI, reflective supervision).  
  * Systems/policy: Develop HIPAA/data-sharing primers, resource directories, shared data platforms/intake hubs, bundled payments (pregnancy → 12 months postpartum), expand mother-and-baby residential capacity, and embed Indigenous leadership/ICWA-informed courts.  
  * Payer strategy: Tie payer pilots to avoided downstream costs (ED, NICU, foster care) and pursue payment parity (including audio-only visits).
